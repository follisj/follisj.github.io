Background

For me, the summer of 1993 was the “Summer of Shakespeare and Steely Dan.” I was an undergraduate, and after a rough spring I needed something different. I picked up A Decade of Steely Dan and—between familiar radio cuts and new discoveries—the album sparked long conversations with friends. I also (somewhat accidentally) found myself enrolled in both World Religions and a Shakespeare course. The idea to connect Shakespeare and Steely Dan didn’t arrive then; it surfaced decades later while experimenting with AI, beginning around February 2024.

I asked multiple AI systems to explore connections—mappings, lineages, and song pairings—and later summarized their outputs and built a web presentation.

The Process

AI systems used: Claude 4.0, Gemini 2.5 Pro, ChatGPT 5, Perplexity, Grok, Mistral, Meta, and Copilot. Scope limited to the first seven Steely Dan studio albums (1972–1980) and Shakespeare’s plays.

Plays → Albums Lineage (Shakespeare → … → Steely Dan) Top 5 Songs ↔ Plays/Soliloquies

Prompts

  • Map Shakespeare plays to the first 7 Steely Dan albums and provide the rationale.
  • Establish a linkage: Shakespeare influenced A, who influenced B, …, who influenced x who influenced Steely Dan.
  • Map the top 5 Steely Dan songs to Shakespeare plays and/or specific soliloquies.

Top 5 songs were allowed to vary by AI (each system selected its own list).

Results

Plays → Albums (Highlights)

Only Hamlet and Macbeth were named by all programs—but not to the same albums. Hamlet was mapped to five different albums (four programs → Pretzel Logic), and Macbeth to four albums (four programs → The Royal Scam).

The most common pairings:

  • The Tempest ↔ Aja — six programs
  • Othello ↔ Katy Lied — five programs
  • A Midsummer Night’s Dream ↔ Can’t Buy a Thrill — four programs

Why The Tempest ↔ Aja?

Claude: late-style mastery; magic/otherworldliness; control/orchestration; resolution & reconciliation.

ChatGPT: late-style mastery (final play vs. peak album); serene transcendence; Prospero-like studio perfectionism.

Countdown to Ecstasy ≈ many plays

The album was mapped to seven different plays across AIs (3 comedies, 4 tragedies), signaling a flexible thematic profile.

Agreement & Similarity

  • Highest agreement: Gemini ↔ Copilot (6/7), differing only on Countdown to Ecstasy.
  • Copilot ↔ Perplexity agreed on 5/7 (swapping King Lear/Macbeth across The Royal Scam/Gaucho).
  • Gemini ↔ Perplexity agreed on 4/7 with a similar Lear/Macbeth swap and different Countdown choices.
  • Least agreement: Mistral and Meta; both had multiple unique pairings and fewer overlaps.

Lineage (Shakespeare → … → Steely Dan)

Most programs proposed a single path; ChatGPT offered six, including the shortest: Shakespeare → Duke Ellington → Steely Dan. Across the non‑ChatGPT outputs, the average number of intermediaries was four.

  • Most common influences: William S. Burroughs and T. S. Eliot (each named by four of the seven non‑ChatGPT systems).
  • Also recurring: John Donne, Oscar Wilde, Vladimir Nabokov, James Joyce, Duke Ellington, Bob Dylan, Tom Lehrer.
  • Odd outlier cited by Meta: Jay McInerney (later‑era tie to Fagen).

All ChatGPT lineages overlapped with others only on Burroughs, Eliot, Wilde, and Nabokov.

Final Thoughts

Likely the first sustained attempt (found via AI search) to connect Shakespeare with Steely Dan, this project shows both convergence and variability across AI systems. The mappings often rhyme, but rationales diverge; lineages crisscross through different cultural figures. Next steps could include a deeper manual review and personal curation of definitive pairings.

If I could revisit the summer of 1993, this would’ve made a lively Shakespeare project—especially with today’s tools: AI research companions and a streaming catalog a click away.
